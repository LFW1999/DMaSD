Introduction

A task was set at the beginning of the module to produce virtual artefacts with the potential of making them physical through the use of a multitude of techniques studied in the time between. A budget of 50GBP pp was allotted, for a total of 150GBP per team of three. For the design, our team decided to work on developing a model of a Tyrannosaurus Rex (T-Rex). We felt that it would be an optimal design choice to allow us to include a variety of different techniques, such as utilising the resin lab for a skin texture, and 3D printing for harder textures (such as teeth) and for creating components to allow for motion in the jaw, using a joint.
The following sections on this page will explore the initial design phase and the techniques available that were considered, as well as a critical analysis of areas where similar techniques have been employed, to deecide if it was useful to our design.
Please note that, in the interest of maintaining a compact and clean design, IEEE style referencing will be used. The Bibliography page will have its own section dedicated to RMADSD, where you can find all references in one place. If time allows, each reference will have its own hyperlink. A Bibliography will be included regardless.

Figure 1, which shows the initial sketch of the design as well as a completed example [1], outlined the aim of the design at the initial stage. Whilst a model as detailed as the example on the right was deemed unattainable (due to a combined lack of skill, cost of production, and limitations of hardware), it was reasoned that a simpler skull design could be supplemented with additional features to create a complex artefact in a different regard. The group decided that the artefact could be wall-mounted, adding a neck to the model as an anchor, and that the jaw could be fitted with bearings/joints to allow for some movement. It was also discussed that a motor could be used if the design timeline allowed for it. The motor would be hidden in the neck, with gears being 3d printed to allow asynchronous movement of the jaw and rotation of the head on a pivot from the top of the neck. Furthermore, discussion was had over adding skin to the model. This was decided to be done using resin, but was apparent from the start of the design phase that covering the whole model may be too expensive. It was for this reason that the design opted to cover half of the skull in skin, giving it an x-ray view. Whilst it was also debated in having a moving eye on the side covered with resin, this feature was dropped as to not overload the initial design process and allow theoreticals to outstretch deliverables.
